Step 1. Define the Research Goal

Before diving into tools, decide what you’re trying to learn and who you want to compare. A focused goal helps the AI return answers you can actually use.

1. Get clear on the basics

  • What question am I trying to answer?
  • Who are the main and smaller players?
  • What do I need to compare — features, prices, audience, or integrations?
  • How should the AI show the results — a list, table, or short summaries?

2. Tell the AI exactly what you want. You can control the format, tone, and amount of detail right in your prompt.

“Act as a market researcher. For the category [your category], list 8–12 competitors (include 3 smaller or niche ones). Return the results in a table with these columns: | Name | One-line description | Target user | Top 3 features | Price range. Use clear, neutral language and include any sources if available.”

Want to explore connected tools? Add:

“Also note any related or partner tools these companies integrate with.”

Tip: Start your scan in Perplexity — it gives you live, cited results from the web, so you can see where each claim comes from and stay current with new releases. Once you’ve gathered the data, use ChatGPT to cluster insights, format tables, and clean the language for presentation.

Step 2. Gather Competitor Data

Collect structured evidence across official sites, pricing pages, release notes, help centers, App Store/Play reviews, and Reddit threads. Ask AI to extract comparable fields.

Fields to capture

  • Core features & workflows (bulleted, not marketing copy)
  • Pricing model & limits (tiers, MAUs, seats, credits, overages)
  • Integrations & ecosystems (POS, CRM, analytics, file types)
  • Recent launches/roadmap signals (last 12–18 months)
  • User sentiment themes with representative quotes (praise/complaints)

Extraction prompts

“From these URLs, extract feature inventory, pricing structure, integrations, and release highlights (with dates). Return a compact table. Include source links for each cell.”
“Summarize top 6 praise and top 6 complaints from App Store/Reddit for [competitor]. Group by theme and add one short quote per theme.”

Deliverables

  • Competitor factsheet per product (one page)
  • Unified feature table (rows = features, columns = competitors)
  • Pricing snapshot with notes on fine print and constraints

Step 3. Identify Patterns and Gaps

Cluster, compare, and label the landscape. Separate “parity features” from real differentiators. Weight findings by frequency and evidence quality.

Analysis moves

  • Cluster features into capability groups (e.g., onboarding, automation, integrations, reporting).
  • Mark table‑stakes vs. differentiators vs. experiments.
  • Map pain points to the competitors that trigger them most often.
  • Score each competitor on fit for target user, complexity, and switching friction.

Pattern prompts

“Using the feature table and sentiment themes, group capabilities into 4–6 clusters. For each, note which competitors lead, which lag, and where users are still creating workarounds.”
“Given these clusters, identify 5 white‑space opportunities and 5 risky me‑too areas. Return as two lists with rationale.”

Output: a one‑page ‘State of the Category’ summary and a short list of opportunity bets.

Step 4. Create the Presentation

Translate analysis into a crisp narrative. Keep it decision‑oriented and cite sources where it matters.

Suggested deck (6–8 slides)

  1. Category snapshot (who, what, why now)
  2. Competitor map (leaders, challengers, niche)
  3. Feature parity vs. differentiation matrix
  4. User sentiment themes (praise/complaints with 1‑line quotes)
  5. Pricing & packaging patterns (gotchas called out)
  6. Opportunities & risks (white space vs. me‑too traps)
  7. Positioning options (2–3 concise directions)
  8. Next steps (tests, interviews, proof points)

Prompt

“Turn the analysis into an 8‑slide outline with slide titles, 3 bullet points each, and a short speaker note. Keep tone neutral and evidence‑based.”

Step 5. Reflect and Document

Harden the insights and bake the workflow into your practice.

Quality checks

  • Bias scan: ask AI to list likely blind spots (dominant voices, underserved users, vendor marketing).
  • Citation spot‑check: open 3–5 sources and confirm the claim language.
  • Replicability: can another teammate reproduce your table from your prompts?
